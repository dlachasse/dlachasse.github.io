Previous work experience

I’ve spent the past 6 years working at a small online apparel (Blank Apparel) retailer selling exclusively on Amazon. I’ve had the chance to do things from printing t-shirts to desiging, building and implementing a proprietary warehouse inventory system.

The position has provided me my first opportunity to program. When I first took the position I was tasked with building huge text inventory files in Excel. I began to automate simple actions with Visual Basic and slowly worked my way up to more complicated patterns.

I also have learned quite a bit of SQL knowledge. Our product database is stored on a SQL Server database and I'm running queries every day — often discovering new functions to retreive the data I need.

After being exposed to these aspects of our business, I found there was so much I could do through automation. In learning to program, I was providing myself a variety of tools that could quickly accomplish tasks that would take an individual an inordinate amount of time.

Fortunately for me, there was the Feedzirra library. It effectively handles large amounts of feeds. I was able to set it up with Resque and a cron job to continuously pull in new articles from followed feeds. I also learned to import and parse opml files with Nokogiri.

Inventory Syncronizer

At my current job, we sell exclusively on Amazon and we do a lot of drop-shipping. We were having a lot of issues accidentally overselling items that weren’t actually in stock due to outdated inventory information.

At the time, the process of uploading an inventory file consisted of manually importing and modifying huge csv files in Excel. It was a hassle to say in the least, and not nearly timley enough. I had been learning Ruby and thought I might take a little bit of time to see if I could automate at least a part of the process.

All of these files are accessed differently — whether that be navigating a website, pulling via ftp or ftps via tls. After much trial and error, I was able to get them all to import properly. Since I had made it that far, I decided I'd try to follow through and automate the whole process. The utility now pulls down, parses the csv, injects values into our database and pushes back out to Amazon’s Inventory api.
